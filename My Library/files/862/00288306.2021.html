ABSTRACT

The cataclysmic basaltic eruption of Mt. Tarawera in 1886 represents a significant cultural and scientific event for New Zealand. This review utilises published and new observations, to reinterpret eruptive parameters encompassing the entirety of the eruption. Introduction

The 1886 eruption of Mt. Tarawera is one of relatively few known basalt eruptions from the rhyolite-dominated central Taupo Volcanic Zone (Figure 1; e.g. Zellmer et al. 2020; Barker et al. 2020), and one of only two basaltic eruptions in New Zealand witnessed by MÄori and European colonists (along with the â¼500 ybp eruption of Rangitoto; Leonard et al. 2017). It was a significant cultural and geologic event in New Zealandâs history (Lowe 2008). Maori legend tells of a phantom canoe observed on Lake Tarawera 10 days prior to the eruption. The canoe, with a design unlike any other on the lake, observed by guide, rowers, and tourists alike, was said to be transporting the souls of the dead to the mountain, portending a coming disaster. Legend suggests that the eruption was an act of vengeance by RÅ«aumoko (unborn child of PapatÅ«Änuku â Earth mother), atua (God) of earthquakes and volcanoes. The eruption cost the lives of â¼120 people, all but seven of whom were indigenous MÄori, including the entire populations of Te Ariki, Moura, Totarariki, Toiniho and Waitangi (with a single exception; Lowe et al. 2001; Keam 2016). The local landscape was also significantly changed over the course of the eruption, with the destruction of the world-famous Pink and White Terraces (Å-tÅ«-kapua-rangi and Te Tarata, respectively), formation of the new Lake Rotomahana, opening of the Chasm and cratering on Mt. The Tarawera eruption represents an explosive end-member to basaltic volcanism with both highly explosive phreatomagmatic and basaltic plinian eruptive styles occurring during the brief, â¼4 hr, eruption. Highly explosive basaltic eruptions are much less common than their felsic equivalents because of the relatively lower viscosity and volatile content of mafic magmas. There are few deposits known from basaltic plinian eruptions, and even fewer eruptions have been reported first hand (e.g. 122 BC eruption of Mt. Etna and the 1853 and 1986 eruptions of Chikurachki; Coltelli et al. 1998; Belousov et al. 2003). The 1886 eruption of Tarawera is unique however, in producing a basaltic plinian eruption from tens of point source vents along a fissure. Significant variations in eruption style, distribution of eruptive products, and volume of erupted material exist along the fissure, extending from Mt. Tarawera, through present day Lake Rotomahana, to Waimangu (Figure 1). This heterogeneity of eruption styles along the fissure significantly modifies the distribution of pyroclastic material, introducing significant uncertainty into estimates of total erupted volume and mass discharge rates (Pullar and Birrell 1973; Walker et al. 1984), impacting our ability to understand driving mechanisms of this eruption in the context of other basaltic plinian systems.

At Mt. Tarawera, vent and conduit geometry were heavily influenced by fault structures, while position of the water table and pre-existing rhyolite lithologies played major roles in the changing eruption styles observed over the course of the eruption (Walker et al. 1984; Houghton et al. 2004; Kennedy et al. 2010), as did extension of the rift, or fissure, into the swamps and geothermal areas where Lake Rotomahana's phreatomagmatic explosions took place (Keam 1988; Rosseel et al. 2006). Componentry, clast textures, and distribution of volcanic material vary laterally along the fissure, particularly between the vents located on Mt. Tarawera versus those from Lake Rotomahana and Waimangu. Despite along-fissure variations in eruptive processes, a compilation of available bulk compositions from Tarawera and Black Crater (Waimangu) reveal a relatively homogenous basaltic component (Figure 2), regardless of analytical methods (e.g. X-ray fluorescence (XRF) vs inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICPMS)), although no systematic geochemical study exists in the literature (Supp. Table 1, 2; Pond and Smith 1886; Grange 1937; Cole 1966; Cole 1970a; Nairn 1979, 1981; Gamble et al. 1990, 1993; Leonard et al. 2002; Nairn 2002; Nairn et al. 2004; Hiess et al. 2007; Rooney and Deering 2014; Zellmer et al. 2020; and unpublished data from E. Klemetti). Compositions are typical of subduction-modified, or crustally-contaminated, basalt with elevated large ion lithophile element (LILE) relative to high field strength element (HFSE) abundances (Figure 2C). Bulk chemistry suggests the Tarawera basalt is not compositionally âabnormalâ compared to other basaltic eruptions in the Taupo Volcanic Zone (Figure 2) despite the highly explosive nature of the eruption. However, even the earliest analyses of volcanic dust, ash, mud and lapilli, indicate a large component of rhyolitic material derived from the underlying volcanic and hydrothermal system (Pond and Smith 1886; Supp. Table 1).

In this review of a unique New Zealand eruption we explore and compare the two disparate segments of this highly explosive eruption (Mt. Tarawera and Lake Rotomahana-Waimangu), reviewing investigations and reports dating back to the eruption, and integrating new findings from the Lake Rotomahana area, to present a new assessment of eruptive parameters such as mass discharge rates and eruptive volumes. Although prior research suggests fissure/vent geometry and interaction with groundwater are largely responsible for driving this highly explosive eruption, we also integrate new mineral geochemistry from Mt. Tarawera to provide a better understanding of the pre- and syn-eruptive driving forces of the magmatic system that may have influenced this dynamic eruption. As a result of the disparity between the two main rift segments (Mt. Tarawera and Lake Rotomahana), this review treats each segment individually, only integrating for the discussion of eruptive parameters.

Eruption setting and chronology

Mt Tarawera is located at the southeastern end of the Okataina caldera complex. The Okataina caldera complex represents up to 4 different collapse events and associated eruptions of large rhyolitic ignimbrites, dating back to â¼557 ka (Cole et al. 2014). Volcanism within the caldera is predominantly rhyolitic, punctuated with sporadic small volume basaltic volcanism. Mt. Tarawera is constructed from a series of rhyolitic domes erupted over the past â¼22 ky, bisected by the 1886 basaltic eruption (Cole 1970b; Cole et al. 2014). Three of the post-22 ka rhyolitic eruptions from Mt. Tarawera have preserved evidence of basalt interaction shortly before eruption (Shane et al. 2007). Vents for the rhyolitic domes are aligned in a NE orientation known as the Tarawera linear vent zone, subparallel to the Haroharo linear vent zone in the northern half the caldera, indicating a strong structural control to eruptive vent locations. Margins of the Tarawera dome complex delineate the southern expression of the Okataina caldera complex while the western margin of present-day Lake Rotomahana roughly coincides with the western boundary (Cole et al. 2014).

The eruption of Mt. Tarawera began in earnest in the early hours of 10 June 1886. A detailed review of the eruption chronology is provided by Cole (1970b) and Keam (1988, 2016), from which we summarise the salient points. Although times vary based on accounts, the relative order of events is important for understanding the changing dynamics of the eruption. Sustained ground shaking was felt starting at â¼1 am on the 10 June and the eruption began at â¼1:45 am on the northeastern part of Mt. Tarawera. Earthquake activity and an increasing number of erupting vents accompanied the propagation of the fissure towards the southwest until â¼3:30 am. Between 2:10 and 2:30 am activity increased with âa continuous and fearful roarâ (Pond and Smith 1886), and the development of an eruption column estimated to reach 28âkm (Walker et al. 1984). By 2:30 am, the whole length (8âkm) of the fissure across Mt. Tarawera was in eruption, and explosions began at the Waimangu valley, some 6âkm southwest, skipping the low-lying geothermal marshlands of Rotomahana (Keam 1988). At 3:30 am a particularly violent earthquake was felt and witnesses reported that massive dark plumes rose from the Rotomahana/Rotomakariri basin region such that the entire 17-km long fissure was in eruption, producing a raining mud from Rotomahana, mixed in with the pyroclasts from the Mt. Tarawera portion of the fissure and a 10â13âkm eruption column (Figure 1; Hutton 1887; Keam 1988). During the latter stages of the eruption, heavy âshocksâ at â¼4:30 am and â¼5:30 am punctuated groundshaking (Pond and Smith 1886). The main phase of the eruption seems to have terminated by â¼6 am (Hutton 1887; Keam 2016) although steaming and isolated explosions carried on for weeks to months, or in the case of Waimangu, years (Cole 1970b).

Extending approximately 17âkm from Mt. Tarawera to Waimangu, the eruptive rift and explosion craters have a general strike of â¼60Â° ENE, consistent with prior trends for alignment of pre-1886 vents on Mt. Tarawera. The 1886 rift on Mt. Tarawera itself stretches â¼8âkm and comprises 13 main craters with more than 50 vents (Houghton et al. 2004; Sable et al. 2006). Cole and Hunt (1968), using magnetic anomalies, suggest two parallel shallow dikes were emplaced, primarily beneath Mt. Tarawera (not observed beneath Waimangu), although only one is exposed at the surface. Two exposures of basaltic dike in the northeast segment of the fissure range from â¼2âm thick with chilled margins to a complex assembly â¼12âm in expanse (Cole and Hunt 1968). Individual eruptive fissures and additional dike orientations indicate the overall rift pattern is comprised of left-stepping en echelon features at generally 70â80Â° ENE, rotated eastward from the overall rift orientation (Nairn and Cole 1981). The consistency of fissure orientations suggests neither local topography nor the structure of the underlying rhyolitic dome complex had much impact, and instead larger crustal structures and regional stress patterns control the orientation of the fissures (Cole et al. 2010). However, as will be discussed later, shallow pre-existing volcanic products did play a potentially significant role in the explosivity of the eruption. Vent geometry was calculated from the distribution of plinian pyroclastic material, yielding an average vent width of 18 Â± 3âm (at surface). Vents extend for â¼4âkm through the main fissure to the northeast (Walker et al. 1984), a length consistent with the relatively narrow dikes observed at depth within the fissures (Cole and Hunt 1968).

While on Mt. Tarawera itself and through the Waimangu basin the fissure is narrow, the middle section, through the Rotomahana/Rotomakariri basin, hosts a series of coalesced maars now filled and covered by modern-day Lake Rotomahana (Simmons et al. 1993; Wood 1994) (Figures 1 and 3). Before the eruption, the Rotomahana/Rotomakariri basin was home to two small shallow marshy lakes, Lake Rotomahana and Lake Rotomakariri, and a highly active geothermal system hosting hot springs, fumaroles, and the by then already internationally known Pink and White Terraces, stepped sinter formations renowned for their natural beauty.

Eruption deposits

All erupted juvenile pyroclasts from the 1886 eruption are basaltic and the eruption produced four geographically distinct products (Houghton and Wilson 1998):

  1. a widespread scoria fall bed covering at least 10,000 km2, erupted from northeastern craters (Mt. Tarawera).

  2. a coarse-grained cone-forming scoria fall deposit confined to within c. 400âm of the northeastern half of the Tarawera rift.

  3. a widespread deposit rich in fine, lithic-dominated, ash (the âRotomahana Mudâ) erupted from the south-west half of the rift (mainly Rotomahana and to a lesser extent Waimangu)

  4. an apron of thicker âRotomahana Mudâ deposits enclosing the eruption craters of Rotomahana, emplaced largely by pyroclastic density currents (âsurgesâ).

The regional distributions of (i), (ii) and (iii) were broadly mapped immediately after the eruption and published as a composite isopach map by Thomas (1888) (Figure 4). Pullar and Birrell (1973) then distinguished the distribution of (i) and (iii) in their regional compilation of Quaternary tephra deposits highlighting the northerly dispersal of the Rotomahana Mud while the scoria deposit extends northeast of the vent area. Nairn (1979) established that proximal deposits of (iv) were from surges, and Walker et al. (1984) remapped the widespread scoria fall (i) and determined that it originated from Mt. Tarawera.

There are clear differences in the described distribution of the whole deposit between the measurement periods. Both Thomas (1888) and Pullar and Birrell (1973) map the combination of Rotomahana Mud and Mt. Tarawera scoria fall deposits, and the overall deposit footprint is similar displaying two lobes with axes oriented N-S and NE-SW. Winds affecting the ash distribution of tephra were initially light from the south, shifting at about 4 am to blowing strongly from the southwest (Thomas 1888). This change of wind direction and strength occurred when vents along Rotomahana were in eruption and a high plume was present, suggesting a northerly dispersal of the Rotomahana Mud relative to the northeasterly dispersal for Mt. Tarawera scoria. Both Thomas (1888) and Pullar and Birrell (1973) also indicate the extent of the deposit and a âvolcanic dustâ region, which is largely derived by observations from Bay of Plenty to Hawkeâs Bay. It is unclear what the deposit density or thickness of this may represent. The Walker et al. (1984) isopach map has a much smaller deposit footprint than Thomas (1888) and Pullar and Birrell (1973). Two lobes can be discerned in the medial deposits relating to the 100 and 50âmm deposit isopachs, but isopachs of lesser thickness are largely circular.

Mt. Tarawera scoria fall deposits

The 1886 medial-distal fall deposits display complex stratigraphy, reflecting diverse transport histories of clasts erupted from tens of vents that were in different styles of eruption along a fissure source. The deposits reflect contributions of both (i) and (iii) as described above from a 17âkm-long fissure with more than 50 vents. Having vents active at different times during the eruption resulted in vastly different eruption styles which complicated the dispersal patterns. A further complication was introduced by changes of wind speeds and directions throughout the event.

Medial/distal stratigraphy displays a moderately-well sorted lower scoria fall deposit, which transitions upwards to a scoria fall deposit with â¼<5% Rotomahana Mud, and further upwards transitions to scoria fall deposits with a much higher abundance (â¼20%) of the Rotomahana Mud. The incoming of the Rotomahana Mud into the deposit stratigraphy varies depending on location (Supp. Fig. 6).

The first modern physical volcanology study of the 1886 proximal pyroclastic succession on Mt. Tarawera was that of Walker et al. (1984); deposit and clast characteristics, and eruption dynamics have been studied a number of times subsequently (e.g. Sable et al. 2006; Carey et al. 2007; Sable et al. 2009). Houghton and Wilson (1998) and Houghton et al. (2004) established a modified stratigraphy (based on the original 5 units defined by Walker et al. 1984), with three units for the northeastern segment of the fissure, where the lowermost deposit (unit 1) is phreatomagmatic, followed by a thick magmatic sequence (unit 2/3), then capped by a final phreatomagmatic unit (unit 4/5). This stratigraphy is carried forward in this review. Phreatomagmatic units 1 and 4/5 have a higher abundance of lithics and blocky juvenile clasts with low to moderate vesicularity often with dense rims. Clast morphology and high lithic content are suggestive of water-magma interaction (White and Valentine 2016), and Houghton et al. (2004) infer that while water was involved, the amount was small. Magmatic unit 2/3 is oxidised, and consists of well-bedded layers of ragged scoria with moderate clast densities (Houghton et al. 2004). Distinctive pale muddy deposits from Rotomahana punctuate the upper half of this stratigraphy, consistent with the later initiation of eruption in the Rotomahana segment of the rift (Walker et al. 1984).

Lateral distribution of crater rim deposits

Houghton et al. (2004) indicate that the timing of the transitions between phreatomagmatic and magmatic eruption styles occurred at different times along the rift. The unit 2/3 deposits on crater walls along the fissure can be separated into packages of contrasting dispersal (Sable et al. 2006). Defining t1/2 as a linear half-thickness of individual tephra packages and correlated to eruption intensity (higher t1/2 equates to higher intensity eruption), field observations indicate a wide range of values, from t1/2 <20âm to â¼280âm (Sable et al. 2006; Figure 5). The maximum thickness of proximal tephra deposits along the fissure is estimated at â¼70âm (Sable et al. 2006). Walker et al. (1984) suggested evenly distributed accumulation of deposits along the fissure provides key evidence that the plinian eruption utilised the entire length of the rift rather than a single point source. The wide range of t1/2 at various craters along the Tarawera rift suggests, however, that not all vents erupted with the same intensity (Figure 5). Overlapping and intermingled tephra beds along crater walls do however support observations that multiple vents were active at a given time (Houghton et al. 2004; Sable et al. 2006). Sable et al. (2006) conclude that vents contributing more significantly to the plinian plume were probably confined to four of the thirteen main craters (F,H,J,L; Figure 1) distributed unevenly from the centre to the southwest rift segments on Mt. Tarawera, where t1/2 values are the greatest. Craters dominated by bed packages with lower t1/2 values are consistent with vents that were erupting in lower-intensity activity.

Microtextures

Investigations of juvenile clast microtextures from both a widespread (t1/2 >80âm) and a localised (t1/2 <50âm) proximal deposit suggest the juvenile basaltic component had a shared history up until fragmentation at different craters and vents, despite differences in eruption intensity (Sable et al. 2009). Vesicle number densities of juvenile clasts are relatively constant between the two sections at â¼106 cmâ3, with vesicles dominantly between 0.02 and 3âmm in diameter (Sable et al. 2009). Vesicles are polylobate-shaped rather than the spherical form commonly seen in strombolian eruptions, attesting to the high viscosity of the magma and impact of dense microlite population on bubble dynamics (Figure 7). Sable et al. (2009) show that all studied unit 2/3 and medial plinian juvenile clasts have roughly the same microlite distribution (85â99 vol%). Crystallinities determined by X-ray diffraction reported by Wall et al. (2014) support such high microlite crystallinities within the medial plinian clasts, however analyses by Wall et al. (2014) and additional new analyses following the same methodology, suggest groundmass crystallisation may vary between clasts from the proximal eruptive units (Supp. Fig. 1). Clasts collected from crater F units 1 and 4/5 all indicate identical groundmass crystallinities, ranging from 80 to 95 vol%. However clasts from proximal unit 2/3 in crater F have groundmass crystallinities ranging from 47 to 78 vol%, significantly lower than observed in other units, and lower than the unit 2/3 clasts reported in Sable et al. (2009). Interestingly, groundmass crystallinity is not correlated with clast size in phreatomagmatic units 1 and 4/5. However, although variable, unit 2/3 and the medial plinian deposit show increases in groundmass crystallisation with increasing clast size from 0 to 5.3 Ï (1â40âmm on the long axis). Although sample numbers are limited, this may suggest that post-fragmentation crystallisation was halted or limited in the phreatomagmatic deposits while microlite growth continued post-fragmentation and during emplacement of magmatic deposits.

Mineralogy and geochemistry

A significant aspect of componentry is a detailed understanding of the mineralogy of the eruption. The mineralogy of the Mt. Tarawera eruption has the potential to preserve key details of the pre-eruptive magmatic history and may give further insight into the driving forces (such as magma degassing) of such a significant volcanic event. However, a modern, and thorough mineralogical study of the Mt. Tarawera eruption does not exist in the literature. Here we present new data of mineral compositions and trapped, co-existing melt inclusions to (1) provide context for basaltic magma interaction with local crustal materials, (2) provide additional constraints on P-T conditions of crystallisation, (3) assess evidence for magma mixing and melt evolution, and (4) assess the pre-eruptive volatile budget of the magma. These mineralogical and geochemical parameters allow us to understand magmatic conditions prior to, and during the eruption, and assess the impact of volatile degassing on the eruption. New analyses are from medial tephra samples (T1, T2, T13-2b) as well as proximal samples of the lower phreatomagmatic (unit 1; T13-4), climactic (unit 2/3; T13-5), and upper phreatomagmatic (unit 4/5; T13-6) phases from crater F as described above (Supp. Tables 3-6). Analytical methods are described in the Supplemental Methods. Glassy melt inclusions are observed and analysed in all mineral phases from the medial plinian sample, excluding biotite, and elucidate compositional variations, supporting petrogenic classifications of minerals as xenocrysts, antecrysts or phenocrysts. Major element compositions of melt inclusions reported here (Supp. Table 3) supplement those reported by Barker et al. (2020) and unpublished data by E. Hughes. New clinopyroxene and orthopyroxene-hosted melt inclusion compositions reported here are corrected for post-entrapment crystallisation based on Fe-Mg equilibrium, while feldspar and quartz-hosted inclusions are uncorrected (see supplemental methods for further details on melt inclusion textures and corrections). Inclusion compositions range from 50 to 77âwt% SiO2, and are largely bimodal (Figure 2A), reflecting the interaction of the basaltic magma with shallow rhyolitic crust (e.g. rhyolitic (71â77âwt% SiO2) melts are preserved in abundant quartz xenocrysts). Inclusions of basaltic melt, similar to the juvenile Tarawera basalt, are found in olivine, clinopyroxene, and high-Ca plagioclase.

The groundmass is dominated by plagioclase (â¼57%) and clinopyroxene (â¼40%), with the remainder olivine and Fe-Ti oxides (Figure 7; Sable et al. 2009). Phenocrysts are rare, making up <0.5 vol% of the Tarawera basalt, which is often characterised as aphyric, despite a highly crystallised groundmass (85â99 vol% microlite). However, as discussed above, xenocrystic material is observed throughout the basalt. Mineral phases ((micro)phenocryst and xenocryst) are typically smaller than â¼500 Î¼m. We qualitatively distinguish microphenocrysts from microlites based on our ability to separate individual grains during hand-picking. The primary separable mineral phases, comprising antecrysts, xenocrysts and phenocrysts, in order of relative abundance, include Na-rich plagioclase, quartz, clinopyroxene, olivine, biotite, Ca-rich plagioclase and orthopyroxene. Detailed chemistry of minerals and their melt inclusions for all separated mineral phases from a distal sample as well as clinopyroxene from proximal samples, provide insight into the crystallisation and assembly history of these eruptive units. All mineral analyses are reported in Supp. Tables 3â6 and detailed analytical methodologies are provided in the Supplemental Methodology.

Magmatic volatiles

Walker et al. (1984) suggest that the high eruptive jet velocity (250â350âm/s) was driven by an expanded volatile component of 1.5â3âwt%, if predominantly H2O. However, unpublished analyses of H2O and CO2 in clinopyroxene-hosted inclusions (summary provided by E. Pressures and temperatures of crystallisation

Magmatic temperatures are calculated from clinopyroxene-melt thermobarometry, using melt inclusions and their host pyroxenes (Putirka 2008; Neave and Putirka 2017). Assuming a maximum H2O content of 4.5âwt% (from water in clinopyroxene calculations), calculated temperatures average 1050âÂ±â17 Â°C at 0.8âÂ±â1.4 kbar. In comparison, using the average H2O content of 1.4âwt% H2O, only increases the average temperature and pressure to 1090âÂ±â19Â°C and 1.2âÂ±â1.5 kbar. Overall clinopyroxene thermobarometry suggests generally low magmatic temperatures <1100 Â°C and relatively shallow crystallisation pressures (<2 kbar). Further decreasing the magmatic H2O content to approximate the proximal plinian clinopyroxenes has a negligible impact on P-T estimates. Using the H2O and CO2 from melt inclusion compositions (Hughes unpublished data) provides an estimated pressure range of approximately 0.7â3.1 kbar during clinopyroxene growth (1.8 kbar average saturation pressure; Newman and Lowenstern 2002). This is within the same pressure range as observed from the clinopyroxene-melt thermobarometry. However, CO2âH2O saturation pressures are considered to be a minimum since any CO2 in the shrinkage bubbles has not been accounted for. Results suggest that high magmatic H2O contents were maintained to relatively low pressures beneath Mt. Tarawera prior to the eruption. Interestingly, the crystallisation depths implied for the basaltic magma coincide with prior estimates for storage and crystallisation of rhyolitic magmas in the Okataina caldera complex (Johnson et al. 2011; Cole et al. 2014). Basalts have been identified in three of the past four rhyolitic eruptions from Tarawera (post 22 ka), suggesting these hot mafic magmas commonly interact with shallowly-stored silicic magma (Shane et al. 2007). However, unlike the prior eruptions, although abundant rhyolitic material is incorporated into the basalt (Figures 2A, 6F, 7), the dike did not initiate the eruption of shallowly stored rhyolitic magmas.

Lake Rotomahana pyroclastic deposits

By 3:30 am on 10 June, the low-lying Rotomahana region joined Mt. Tarawera in eruption. The Rotomahana Mud as a unit is characterised by an abundance of material that is granulometrically mud (particles smaller than 4Ï; < 63âÂµm). As primary volcaniclastic deposits, this material could also be called extremely fine ash (White and Houghton 2006), but the dominance of crushed country rock with clay, pale colour, and deposit cohesion all support continued use of the term âRotomahana Mudâ for the deposits.

Nairn (1979) first described and interpreted base-surge deposition of the proximal Rotomahana Mud, and inferred that the surges were deposited from high velocity, steam-fluidised density currents that reached at least 6âkm west from the source and surmounted steep topography up to 350âm above the vent site. He concluded that juvenile material was definitely erupted in the Waimangu region, and that at the very least magmatic energy from the underlying 1886 dike contributed to the Rotomahana explosions, but did not definitively say whether juvenile material was erupted at Rotomahana. Rosseel et al. (2006) demonstrated that the northeastern portion of Rotomahana erupted juvenile material in the form of highly complex âcoredâ and âloadedâ bombs, and further inferred from bomb characteristics that the conditions in the vent region for the bombs were thermally and physically heterogeneous, with much ejecta falling back into the vent(s) and being re-ejected in composite form. Wood (1994) has outlined the physical features of the modern geothermal systems in the Waimangu area and Simmons et al. (1993) describe changes to the Rotomahana-Waimangu geothermal system following the eruption. Acoustic mapping of the floor of modern-day Lake Rotomahana (de Ronde et al. 2016) revealed previously unknown volcanic features, but focused on the two modern day geothermal systems. One is a continuation of the pre-1886 system that produced the Pink and White Terraces, the other a post-1886 system slightly to the southeast (southwest of Patiti Island; Figure 1). Pittari et al. (2016) identified lithic fragments in Rotomahana ejecta as rhyolites and ignimbrite, some heavily altered, and concluded that rhyolite fragments do not match with subaerially exposed domes or flows in the vicinity of the craters. They are probably from pre-Matahina caldera rhyolites, or other non-exposed older units, suggesting that the ignimbrite fragments are from the 0.32 Ma Matahina ignimbrite or older unexposed units. The degree of alteration of some lithic fragments suggests that the Pink and White Terrace geothermal system was a long-lived system.

In the following sections we provide new observations and measurements of Rotomahana volcanic deposits including sectoral descriptions of facies variations, granulometry and componentry, allowing for comparison and distinction from the Mt. Tarawera scoria described above. These new observations are critical for an assessment of the eruption mechanisms (e.g. magma-water interaction) and the contribution of the Rotomahana deposits to the overall eruption rates and volumes of erupted juvenile basalt.

Deposit description

Here we provide new descriptions for the Rotomahana volcanic deposits. Deposits from Rotomahana, colloquially termed the Rotomahana Mud (Nairn 2002), comprise, in near-source sites, a stack of poorly defined beds, dominated by mud-coated lithic and juvenile ash, lapilli and bombs in a matrix of mud. The âmudâ consists of disaggregated and comminuted material dominated by very small, cohesive particles. There is some variation in âmuddinessâ of the unit, with deposits in the southwest derived from country rock geothermally altered to different degrees, whereas that in the northeast is derived from pumiceous material and basaltic scoria (Figure 10B,C). Juvenile basaltic clasts are pervasive throughout the deposit, in every bed, though varying in shape, texture and granulometry. The beds cannot be correlated over even short distances around the lake margins, suggesting that the vent processes and distributing pyroclastic density currents were similar across multiple vents, and repeated through the eruption. With distance the deposits thin and merge into horizons of mud-coated particles and thin muddy partings that are the Rotomahana Mud, within the more-widespread Tarawera fall deposits. Current indicators such as dunes dominate deposits exposed at tens to hundreds of metres beyond the lake and led to Nairn's (1979) conclusion of emplacement by surges (dilute pyroclastic density currents). The coatings on Tarawera-derived pyroclasts and muddy horizons in deposits otherwise dominated by Mt. Tarawera fall, demonstrate that significant quantities of Rotomahana-derived fines entered a plume that either merged with that from Mt. Tarawera, or possibly drifted beneath it to coat Tarawera clasts as they fell to the ground.

Eastern sector deposits are dominated by beds a few tens of centimetres to a metre thick of framework-supported black vesicular basaltic scoria, with â¼30â50% rhyolitic lithic grains. Based on the relative paucity of fines, and good sorting, these are inferred to be fall deposits. They are interleaved with thick layers of brown lithic-dominated Rotomahana Mud, which is structureless with minor juvenile and lithic lapilli. There are also beds with characteristics transitional between these two. There are local well-defined concentrations of basaltic lapilli in a sand matrix, and relatively well-defined layers or lenses of mostly clast-supported basaltic lapilli that are enclosed in relatively coarse ash to fine sand. The fall deposits represent magmatic eruptive phases from a NE Rotomahana vent near the foot of Mt. Tarawera, and show little evidence for involvement of external water. In contrast the brown mud deposits are inferred to have been produced from a separate Rotomahana vent in which magma-water explosions strongly excavated the substrate.

Western sector deposits of the Rotomahana Mud were studied in outcrops around Lake Rotomahana, and in the valleys and on ridges west of the crater where in â¼2007 forestry operations created outcrops. Equivalent deposits are inferred to surround southern Lake Rotomahana, but are elsewhere not exposed. Western proximal deposits exposed at the lake edge are generally indistinctly stratified and poorly organised, grading into better-stratified deposits further from the lake edge and at higher elevations. Rare better-stratified deposits occur among the less structured ones at the lakeshore, but are more common at medial distances. Among these are the prominent cross-stratified lapilli deposits, comprising interfingering wedge-shaped beds, wavy beds, and lenses of mainly basaltic lapilli that filled scours. They are inferred to have formed from intermittently erosive flows, by deposition from traction transport. The medial and distal deposits differ from proximal ones, but we emphasise that variation is not only with distance from source, but also with position in valleys and on ridges rising away from the lake basin.

Thick to very thick beds, variably well-defined, of mud-rich deposits with large blocks and scattered intermediate sized (â1 to â4Ï) particles have extremely poor sorting. They crop out along the full length of the Rotomahana fissure, with large blocks particularly numerous at northern sites near Green Lake. Bedding sags show that some large blocks were emplaced ballistically. Some beds have lapilli concentrations, ranging from very subtle to prominent and reversely graded, and local lenses of coarse material. Elsewhere there is crude stratification of lapilli concentrations and small blocks in a matrix of fine to very fine lithic-dominated ash.

These deposits are dominant near eruptive centres, and the very poor sorting and abundance of fine particles suggest rapid deposition from high-concentration suspensions without sorting or traction (Sohn and Chough 1989; Chough and Sohn 1990). Lenses of coarse material suggest either that the currents carried coarser-grained pulses, or that showers of ballistic debris entered the moving currents. Inverse grading suggests minor traction transport and development of bedload sheets (Whiting et al. 1988; Bennet and Bridge 1995).

Beyond the sectoral deposits, Rotomahana Mud forms distal surge and fall deposits intercalated with Tarawera scoria. These distal mud deposits consist of extremely fine to coarse lithic-dominated ash and represent the âbackground noiseâ during emplacement of the Rotomahana surges. It is present almost everywhere along the deposit trend, locally hosts accretionary and armoured lapilli, and extends well beyond the limits of the base surge deposits. The lack of internal structure and broad distribution suggests deposition from sustained low-intensity fallout.

Deposit componentry

In contrast to the lack of differences in granulometry in different deposit sectors as defined here, the componentry of the Rotomahana Mud deposits varies noticeably. In the west is the Rotomahana Basin area, dominated by lithic fragments that are mainly highly altered, mostly rhyolitic, fragments that are not distinguished from one another in componentry. To the east is the Waingongongongo/Rotomakariri Basin area, where lithic fragments include ignimbrite, microcrystalline rhyolite, and pumiceous fragments; see Pittari et al. (2016) for a more detailed analysis. The dominance in western deposits of highly altered lithic components is consistent with the pre-eruptive geothermal system having been located in the southwestern pre-eruptive Rotomahana/Rotomakariri basin, an area now largely occupied by the Rotomahana Basin Crater. In most cases the percentage of basalt decreases at finer grain sizes. Pittari et al. (2016) further infer that the weaker strength of these altered rocks in the southwest region might explain why the fissure here is much wider than along the rest of the Tarawera-Rotomahana rift. Componentry was evaluated for 50 samples from 14 proximal locations around the lake, and clasts from â5 Ï to 1 Ï were examined (see Supp. Table 7). 1 Ï (0.5âmm) was determined to be the finest grainsize at which clast rock types could be distinguished with a reasonable margin of error using a binocular microscope. The aims of the componentry analysis were to separate particles into two main groups: juvenile particles and lithic particles (probable wall-rock particles). The characteristics of basalt (dark colour, irregular sub-equant shape, visible vesicularity) made confident identification simple, even for grains covered by a thick coat of mud. Discrimination among wall-rock lithologies was complicated in the finer grain sizes, and by various degrees of mud coating, or by strong alteration of many clasts by pre- or post-eruptive geothermal activity.

Western-sector componentry

The componentry of western samples is dominated by juvenile basalt and highly altered lithic grains. The component distributions are mainly unimodal apart from where large clasts skew the percentages to nearly 100% basalt in the larger grainsize fractions. The grainsize modes for the most part span the â1.0 to â1.5 phi range and the average percentage of basalt is approximately 25% but ranges from 17 to 45% for single grainsize classes (Figure 11).

Eastern-sector componentry

For samples from the eastern sector there are fewer data that would suggest the area was home to a geothermal system. There is little chemical alteration of the lithic fragments and it is easier to identify specific lithic components (Pittari et al. 2016). A key difference is that the basalt percentage is higher than in the west, on average â¼ 85%, but ranging in single grainsize classes from 60 to 100%. As in the western sector, larger grainsize classes are more basalt rich, with the percentage of basalt highest in fine to medium lapilli fractions (â3 and â1.5 phi).

Basalt grains in the western and eastern sectors were all produced during this eruption. They are all pyroclasts; no fragments of older lavas have been recognised in this category. The smaller proportion of basalt in the west may reflect the larger size of the Rotomahana Basin Crater. The greater width and depth of the crater there suggests that the low juvenile proportion reflects a combination of low local magmatic output and effective excavation, perhaps as the result of more-effective wall-rock fragmentation in the geothermal area. The larger crater itself could indicate larger explosions, more-optimal scaled explosion depths (Valentine et al. 2012), or coalescence of subcraters (Valentine et al. 2015). If larger explosions are the cause, it suggests that phreatomagmatic explosions were boosted by hydrothermal heat or ground conditions where the intensely active geothermal system was disrupted.

Averaging of the basalt component percentages for the entire eruption shows that about 20% of the Rotomahana Mud deposit consists of juvenile basalt pyroclasts from Rotomahana, a large proportion of which are dense and blocky, suggesting interaction with water. A small amount of the basalt is fallout from the Tarawera plinian plume, but most of the basaltic clasts have shapes and textures (Figures 7 and 10) different from those of clasts erupted from vents on Mt. Tarawera.

We have chosen to use the two segment exponential method for the volume calculation of the Rotomahana Mud, as it adequately captures the sharp transition between thinning rate of the proximal surge and medial-distal fall deposits (Figure 13). Tarawera-Rotomahana conclusion

The 1886 Tarawera eruption was unique in many ways. It is one of the few basaltic plinian style eruptions known, as well as one of the few plinian eruptions of any magma composition from a fissure, rather than a point source. The eruption also has a spectrum of behaviour and intensity from different parts of the fissure (Figure 14). Where local topography was high (Mt. Tarawera) the eruption opened with phreatomagmatic explosions and grew in intensity to produce a plinian eruption column from four sites on the fissure (Sable et al. 2006), and lower intensity activity elsewhere along the on-mountain fissure. Measured water concentrations, the first reported for this eruption, and groundmass crystallinity support slight differences between phases of the eruption on Mt. Tarawera, warranting further investigation. In the low-lying areas, which were swampy and in part occupied by an intensely active geothermal system, the onset of eruption was 1â2 h later, and intense phreatomagmatic to hydrothermal eruptions took place (Pond and Smith 1886; Figure 14).

Previous authors inferred that eruption of the main Rotomahana segment in 1886 was either purely phreatic (Nairn 1979; Mastin 1995) or hydrothermal with a magmatic boost (i.e. juvenile material did erupt and contribute to the overall energetics of the eruption, but the majority of the eruption energy came from hydrothermal explosions; Browne and Lawless 2001). The Rotomahana eruption was not phreatic because componentry reveals that a fifth of the deposit consists of juvenile material, consistent with the Rotomahana eruption being significantly larger, hotter and with more-extensive surge deposits than any otherwise comparable, but purely hydrothermal, eruption (Mastin 1995). Nairn (1979) concluded that the basalt clasts in the deposit were fallout from the Tarawera plume, but most basalt clasts within the Rotomahana Mud are dense (1.73 gcmâ3), and show typical magma-water interaction morphologies. There are juvenile clasts from Tarawera within the Rotomahana Mud; they have morphologies matching those from the plinian Mt. Tarawera segment of the eruption, and reflect interaction between the two adjacent plumes.

Below we summarise the main concluding points of the comparison between the Tarawera and Rotomahana rift segments of the 1886 eruption:

  1. Pyroclastic density currents from the Rotomahana segment caused all of the casualties from the eruption, and phreatomagmatic excavation turned an area of shallow lakes, marshes and geothermal features into a single 7âkm long lake mostly 60â100âm deep. Products of this excavation produced deposits with a volume broadly similar to that of the eruption from Mt. Tarawera.

  2. Despite the similar total deposit volumes, and thus potential hazard impact, the volume of juvenile basalt erupted from Rotomahana was a fraction, between 6 and 23% (not uncommon for tephra rings of maar-diatreme deposits; White and Ross 2011), of that from Mt. Tarawera. At Rotomahana, a low magma flux combined with magma-water interactions in wet ground and in a geothermal field produced large amounts of ejecta. Distribution of that ejecta was primarily by density currents (surges; Figure 14), with the result that the low-flux end of the eruption produced the greatest human impact.

  3. Rotomahana produced at least five recognisably distinct craters distributed along about 4.5âkm of the rift. This is a wider spacing than on Mt. Tarawera, but may obscure more closely spaced vents with craters that merged as they grew (e.g. Valentine et al. 2017).

  4. Rotomahana produced a population of basalt pyroclasts distinctly more dense than those from Mt. Tarawera. The mixing of deposits from eastern Rotomahana and fallout from Mt. Tarawera, together with an absence of geochemical differences either along the rift or through time, precludes certainty in distinguishing all basaltic fallout from Mt. Tarawera from potential high-vesicularity particles from vents in the Rotomahana sector (cf. Ort et al. 2018)

  5. Time-averaged magma eruption rates on the 8âkm Tarawera segment were an order of magnitude higher than the 9âkm Rotomahana-Waimangu segment, and the 106â107 kgâsâ1 eruption rates are within the field of historical subplinian-plinian eruptions.
