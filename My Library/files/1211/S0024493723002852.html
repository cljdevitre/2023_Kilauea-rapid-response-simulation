Chemical characterization of a magma recharging and mixing before an eruption: Insights from chronologically constrained melt inclusions

Abstract

Despite the many scientific contributions and advances in our understanding of magmatic processes that have been derived from the analyses of melt inclusions (MI), it is difficult to find examples of chronologically constrained MI, tracking a magma mixing event before an eruption. Here, we studied a single, reversely zoned olivine phenocryst with multiple MI from the Solchiaro eruption on Procida Island (Phlegrean Volcanic District, Southern Italy) to understand both the order of appearance of basaltic versus trachybasaltic magmas and the reliability of MI to record a magma mixing event. Based on phosphorous X-ray mapping, MI are characteristic of three populations: early, intermediate, and late MI. Early MI represent trachybasaltic melts which differentiate by fractional crystallization to form the liquids that are represented by intermediate MI. Late MI are hosted within a forsterite-rich growth band near the olivine rim representing a mixing event between basalts and the pre-existing trachybasaltic magmas shortly before the eruption. The compositions of the three MI populations are compared to a database of bulk rocks and MI from the literature on the mafic products of the Phlegrean Volcanic District to test if the MI studied show same geochemical trends.

In addition, in this study, the late MI represented a melt inclusion assemblage (MIA) sensu stricto that represents a group of MI trapped all along the same growth band of the olivine. This simultaneous entrapment offers the opportunity to study the reliability of MI based on Roedder's rules. Correlations between major element contents and the size of late MI suggest that MI â‰¥22 Î¼m are the most reliable in recording the compositions of the melt at specific times and places in the plumbing system.

1. Introduction

The reliability of melt inclusions (MI) for deciphering magmatic processes is debated (e.g., Cannatelli et al., 2016; Esposito, 2021; Frezzotti, 2001; Lowenstern, 1994; Metrich and Wallace, 2008; Roedder, 1979; Rose-Koga et al., 2021; Sobolev, 1996; Wallace et al., 2021). While the compositional variability of MI in single samples or phenocrysts can nominally be used to understand the magmatic evolution of volcanic systems, the observed variability can potentially result from modification after trapping (e.g., Barth and Plank, 2021; Bucholz et al., 2013; Lloyd et al., 2013) or by entrapment of a melt in disequilibrium with the growing mineral phase (Baker, 2008; Danyushevsky et al., 2004). The key to assessing whether the MI composition is reliable is to study melt inclusion assemblages (Bodnar and Student, 2006). Melt inclusion assemblages represent groups of MI trapped simultaneously and, thus, in the same chemical and physical conditions. Compositional differences among MI in an MIA can therefore guide to (1) interpretation of the modification processes that occurred after entrapment and (2) critical selection of those MI that better represent the melt in equilibrium with the crystal growing in magmas (e.g., Esposito et al., 2014). However, MIAs are rare in phenocrysts and additional approaches must be proposed to interpret data based on MI. One of the keys to studying MI and interpreting data is to recognize distinct populations of MI chronologically constrained hosted in crystals of individual samples or, when possible, in individual crystals (e.g., Esposito, 2021). However, rare are the cases in which the relative age of MI has been rigorously characterized in order to allow interpretation of MI data within a robust chronological framework. For instance, Peppard et al. (2001) used cathodoluminescence to study populations of MI within growth zones of individual quartz phenocrysts of the Bishop Tuff (California). Based on trace element analysis of 20 MI, Ba-enrichment of MI trapped within later growth bands of quartz and the zoning of the host suggest open-system processes during magma differentiation as crystal settling in a chemically stratified reservoir.

At Procida Island, D'Antonio and Di Girolamo (1994) studied eight mafic juvenile vesiculated scorias and five lithic lava clasts found in Solchiaro and Vivara hydromagmatic deposits showing the least evolved volcanic products of the Phlegrean Volcanic District (PVD). The lava lithics are basalts while scorias are trachybasalts based on major element compositions and the TAS diagram (D'Antonio and Di Girolamo, 1994). In addition, De Astis et al. (2004) studied mafic juveniles from Procida deposits reporting a basaltic scoria from Solchiaro deposit as the lithic lava fragments reported by D'Antonio and Di Girolamo (1994). The basaltic whole rocks of Procida all show various degrees of depletion of K2O and also show lower Na2O and P2O5 but higher MgO and CaO and Al2O3/K2O ratios relative to the trachybasaltic juvenile scorias. Mafic compositions reported by over 100 MI analysis hosted in olivine of the same deposits show K2O and Al2O3/K2O partially overlapping with the mafic bulk rocks of lithic and scorias but spanning in a broader range (Esposito et al., 2011; Mormone et al., 2011). The composition range is also shown by MI hosted in olivine of similar forsteritic composition (see Fig. 12 in Esposito et al., 2018). Thus, the K2O enrichment or depletion is not a function of magmatic differentiation as MI hosted in similar Fo mole% olivine still show a wide range of K2O concentration. This is important because the basaltic rocks showing the lowest alkaline compositions at Procida were relegated to lithic clasts, and thus interpreted as precursors of the more alkali-rich (trachybasaltic) magmas. However, based on these recent works, it is more plausible to interpret the degree of K2O enrichment as not dependent on time. Based on bulk rocks of Procida from the literature, Peccerillo (2020) speculate that two types of primary magmas were present below Procida. One type of magma is represented by lithic lava clasts showing less enriched compositions and lower LILE/HFSE ratios (basalts with calc-alkaline affinity). The other type of primary magma is characterized by enriched compositions and higher LILE/HFSE ratios (trachybasalts with alkaline potassic affinity) represented by juvenile mafic scorias. Thus, if we analyze MI which are geochronologically constrained in an individual olivine and if MI show a compositional variation, we could also establish which is the order of appearance of these different melts.

For this study, we analyzed major elements and Cl and S of MI representing three chronologically constrained populations hosted in a euhedral olivine phenocryst. The olivine host is reversely zoned from forsterite 83 mol% (Fo83) in the core and forsterite 87 mol% (Fo87) at the rim, and we selected this olivine to investigate if the hosted MI recorded a mixing process. We compared the compositions of these MI with mafic bulk rocks and MI from the literature of the PVD, intending to establish the order of appearance of basaltic versus trachybasaltic magmas. This also serves as a more general test of the utility of the phosphorous X-Ray host crystal mapping approach to amplify the ability of MI to elucidate complex magmatic processes. In addition, we tested the reliability of compositional data from MI to constrain true liquid compositions, based on an MIA represented by late MI.

2. Geological setting

Along with Campi Flegrei and Ischia Island in Southern Italy, Procida Island is a volcanic field part of the Phlegrean Volcanic District (PVD, Orsi et al., 1996), and was formed by volcanic deposits of five volcanoes (Vivara, Terra Murata, Pozzo Vecchio, Fiumicello, and Solchiaro). The activity of these five volcanoes started with the Vivara volcano between 73 and 55 ka (Vezzoli, 1988), and ended with the Solchiaro activity 23.6 ka ago (Morabito et al., 2014 and references therein). Along with Mt. Somma-Vesuvius and Campanian Ignimbrites found in the Campanian Plain, the PVD is part of the Campanian Volcanic Province (Peccerillo, 2020). At Procida, bulk rocks show from basaltic and trachybasaltic to shoshonitic and trachytic composition. The only rocks with near-primitive mafic compositions of the entire PVD are found in Procida as scorias or lava lithic in Vivara and Solchiaro pyroclastic deposits (D'Antonio et al., 1999; Peccerillo, 2020). Mafic lithic lava clasts show silica oversaturated basaltic composition with affinity to calcalkaline for major element abundances and ratios. Mixing processes associated with rocks representative of the Solchiaro and Vivara eruptions have been invoked. For instance, D'Antonio et al. (1999) suggested that magma mixing beneath the Island of Procida occurred between trachybasaltic magmas and more evolved latitic magmas based on isotopic compositions and trace element concentrations of bulk rocks. More recently, Esposito et al. (2018) suggested that mixing of Solchiaro magmas occurred between two quasi-primitive magmas: a trachybasaltic magma and a basaltic magma based on MI compositions. These researchers also suggested that the trachybasaltic magmas underwent fractional crystallization, based on an analysis comparing bulk rock and MI compositions with predictions using Rhyolite-MELTS (Ghiorso and Gualda, 2015). During fractional crystallization, these magmas mixed with a recharging hotter basaltic magma that likely triggered the eruption of Solchiaro (Esposito et al., 2018). This chronological order was hypothesized based on the distribution of the most reliable bubble-free MI within olivine host and the type of zoning of these olivines (Table 4 in Esposito et al., 2018). Pressures of formation of the MI associated to the two different types of magmas based on H2O-CO2 silicate melt solubility show partial overlapping with the mixed MI tending to record higher pressure. However, the MI relative chronology was not studied by these researchers, and in this work we attempt to assess this relative age.

3. Analytical methods

The sample studied for this work is RESC2 by Esposito et al. (2011) which is representative of base surge deposits produced during the Solchiaro eruption at the Island of Procida (Southern Italy). The olivine was mounted and prepared following Esposito (2021). We used the electron microprobe (EMP) Jeol 8200 at UCLA to analyze the MI and the olivine host. In addition, we performed P X-Ray maps (Milman-Barris et al., 2008) of seven different sections through the olivine to investigate the texture of the olivine during its growth evolution. Most importantly, we used P X-Ray mapping to assess the relative age of MI studied. Before performing the P X-Ray maps, we attempted to expose each MI at its midsection and particular attention was made to positioning the probe spot in the core of the MI. We analyzed the MI for major elements plus S, Cl, P by EMP in 14 MI belonging to the same MIA (late MIA) near the phenocryst rim, and other 7 MI located in the mantle and the core sectors of the olivine (early and intermediate MIA). The relative 1-Ïƒ errors for the major element concentrations of glasses based on counting statistics are as follows: <1.5% for SiO2, FeOtot, MgO, and CaO, and Al2O3; 1â€“5% for TiO2, Na2O, and K2O; 5â€“11% for P2O5; 10â€“28% for MnO and Cl; 8â€“17% for S. To avoid diffusive Na loss, we used an accelerating voltage of 15 kV, a current of 10 nA and a 5 Î¼m spot. We monitored every single analysis for loss or gain of counts per seconds during the analysis. Na and K were measured first and the acquisition time on the peak for Na and K was 10 s and 20 s for the other elements. A higher beam current of 40 nA and a focused spot mode were employed for the olivine composition. The uncertainties of oxide concentrations are <1% for SiO2, FeO and MgO, and between 5 and 20% for CaO, NiO and MnO. For the conditions of the P-X ray maps, we used a 400 nA beam current with a dwell time of 200 ms using a distance from 1 to 2 Î¼m between each analyzed point.

We correct the melt composition for post-entrapment crystallization (PEC) or post-entrapment dissolution (PED) using the software Petrolog3 (Danyushevsky and Plechov, 2011). We assumed NNO buffer as the oxidation state, employ the equations of Ford et al. (1983) to calculate the MgFe partition coefficient between olivine and silicate melt, and assumed FeOtot = 8.00 wt% for the Fe-loss correction.

Finally, we have employed the Rhyolite-MELTS algorithm (Ghiorso and Gualda, 2015) to predict fractional crystallization, using an early MI (ID: CrA2_1: Appendix Table 1) for the initial composition, NNO as a buffer for the oxygen fugacity, and polybaric crystallization starting from 2 kbar at a rate of 12 bar/Â°C. Volatile concentrations were assumed based on the literature's average H2O and CO2 concentrations of reliable bubble-free MI (see Esposito et al., 2018). Melt compositions of this prediction are reported in Appendix file A1.

4. Results

4.1. Petrography

The P-mapping of the olivine shows that the core is characterized by highly irregular P-zoning at length scales as small as 1â€“3 Î¼m. The intermediate part of the olivine is defined by more regular growth bands alternating relatively thick (10â€“200 Î¼m) P-poor olivine bands to relatively thin (1â€“3 Î¼m) P-rich bands (Fig. 1). Toward the rim of the olivine the P-zoning shows an alternation of thin bands of P-rich- and P-poor in correspondence with the Fo87 rim. The preservation of zoning in the core of the crystalâ€” presumably the oldest partâ€”indicates that the total thermal history of the crystal was insufficient to erase P-zoning by diffusion. Thus, these P-maps are a robust record of internal stratigraphy. This record also suggests that the olivine formed in the magma through three different growth stages: irregular nucleation followed by steady growth (e.g., Faure and Schiano, 2005; Shea et al., 2015; Welsch et al., 2013), and growth after a magma mixing event (e.g. Streck, 2008).

We recognize three main types of MI based on (1) optical microscope observations, (2) P X-ray mapping, and (3) major element composition: Early MI, intermediate MI, and late MI. These three main types of MI are also grouped as Early MI population, intermediate MI population, and late MIA. On the one hand, the late MIA can be defined as an MIA sensu stricto (Bodnar and Student, 2006) because all the MI are trapped at the same time along a single olivine growth band. On the other hand, the other two groups of MI are defined as pseudo-MIA or MI populationsâ€”as opposed to true MIAâ€” because MI in the early or intermediate groups were not necessarily trapped at the same time as all other early or intermediate MI and thus they fail to demonstrate the simultaneous entrapment criterion required for MIA. Instead, early and intermediate MI populations were trapped during distinct stages of olivine growth that may have extended over significant intervals of time.

Late MI are found close to the rim of the olivine. They all show spherical to sub-spherical shape, range from âˆ¼3 to âˆ¼35 Î¼m in diameter, and are bubble-free MI (Fig. 1). Late MI are parallel to the olivine faces and are arranged within P and Al olivine zonation (Fig. 1). Late MI are less evolved than the other types of MI consistent with the olivine reverse FeMg zonation. Intermediate MI are found between the core and the late MI; they have negative crystal shapes and range from âˆ¼20 to 200 Î¼m in the longest direction (Fig. 1 B and D). Some Intermediate MI are bubble-free despite their large size. Intermediate MI are located in relatively P-poor growth bands of olivine (Fig. 1 B). In other cases, intermediate MI are located where the P-zonation is irregular within the P-poor growth band (Fig. 1 D). Early MI are found in the core of the olivine; they are sub-spherical to ovoid shape, range from 20 to 80 Î¼m, are associated with chromite inclusions and are bubble-bearing (Fig. 1 C and D). Early MI correlate to complex P-zoning and a sector of the olivine core rich in chromite inclusions (Fig. 1 C and D). One vapor-rich inclusion was found in the core of this olivine, suggesting volatile saturation during the nucleation of the olivine studied, in agreement with H2O and CO2 systematics in other studies of MI hosted in olivine (Esposito et al., 2011; Mormone et al., 2011).

4.2. Major elements

Uncorrected and corrected MI do not show significantly different major element concentrations in agreement with the relatively low degree of PED and PEC (<6%) calculated based on Petrolog3 software. Early MI are trachybasaltic, intermediate MI range from trachybasaltic to shoshonitic, and the late MI plot in the basaltic field on the TAS diagram by Le Bas et al. (1986). Late MI show lower K2O, Al2O3 and P2O5, but higher CaO concentrations relative to earlier and intermediate MI (Appendix Table 1). Early MI show the lowest SiO2 contents and the K2O concentrations are intermediate to the other two types of MI. As described in detail below, Late MI compositions fail to reproduce K2O and P2O5 enrichments and the CaO depletion of early and intermediate MI by crystal fractionation. In fact, simulated crystallization trends assuming MI and bulk rocks show Al2O3/K2O > 10 wt% (see crystallization green, purple and blue trends Fig. 13a by Esposito et al., 2018).

The magnitude of PEC/PED corrections for late MI were 2â€“5% PEC, suggesting some olivine crystallization occurred after trapping of the late MI but prior to eruption, consistent with some degrees of cooling late in the history of this magma. However, most of the intermediate and early MI show PED rather than PEC. Intermediate MI show from 1% PEC to 2% PED. Early MI show from 2 to 5 PED%. This is not surprising: if a hotter magma recharges the magma where the olivine was crystallizing, pre-existing MI should dissolve rather than crystallize the host at the interface (e.g., Esposito, 2020). Late MI forming after the recharging should show PEC as the T decreases before eruption. To avoid any confusion regarding the PEC and PED corrections of MI we used ratios of elements that are incompatible in olivine for investigating the differentiation processes during the crystallization of this olivine (SiO2/CaO vs. Al2O3/K2O; Fig. 2) because ratios of incompatible elements are not affected by small amounts of PEC or PED (Lima et al., 2003). The MI studied plot on the same trend showed by previous MI and bulk rock studies from the literature (Esposito et al., 2018 and references therein). This is best shown by the Si/Ca vs. Al/K diagram which most clearly discriminates the three different MI populations (Fig. 2).

For the late MIA (sensu-stricto), we considered the uncorrected original analysis of MI. The most important result for the late MIA is that some major elements show correlations with MI size (Fig. 3). For instance, MI â‰¥22 Î¼m show the same CaO concentration, while MI <22 Î¼m show a behavior similar to an up-hill diffusive profile (Fig. 3b). This behavior was reported within single MI hosted in olivine from Galapagos Islands and Sequeiro Fracture Zone by Newcombe et al. (2014). MgO, TiO2, and K2O show similar behavior to CaO: MI â‰¥22 Î¼m show the same oxide concentrations within the analytical errors (Fig. 3a). FeO, P2O5, Na2O, SiO2, Cl, and S do not seem to show any correlation with concentrations within the sigma errors of the measurements. This behavior suggests that MI â‰¥22 Î¼m show the same composition. These MI follow Roedder's rules and are the most reliable MI of this MIA and were those considered for comparison to the MI of the other two populations.

5. Discussion

5.1. Recording crystallization and mixing using MI populations

As shown in Fig. 2, the chronologically distinct populations of MI show different chemical compositions. For instance, the early MI (MI in the core) are representative of the melts from which the olivine has started nucleating. The intermediate MI population represents melts evolved by fractional crystallization from early MI. The crystallization from one of the early MI can produce melt as those trapped in the intermediate MI by fractional crystallization based on Rhyolite-MELTS predictions (green trend in Fig. 2). The late MIA are representative of magma mixing between the intermediate melt and another less evolved melt that recharged the plumbing system before eruption. This type of compositional discrimination of MI by relative time of trapping is consistent with the reverse zoning of the host and highlights the fidelity of MI in recording such magmatic open process. In particular, early and late MI show different K2O, Al2O3, P2O5, and CaO concentrations and Al/K ratios, likely generated from two different basaltic magmas. In fact, the two different types of melts (basaltic versus trachybasaltic) are found in MI hosted in olivine with the same high Fo mole% composition (Fo87â€“88; Fig. 12 and 15c in Esposito et al., 2018), suggesting that near-primary magmas at the same differentiation stage contain both basaltic and trachybasaltic melts. Based on this observation, and using our method to constrain the relative time of formation of the MI in the individual olivine, we can assess the order of appearance of these two magmas at least for the environment where this olivine was formed. Thus, the olivine originated first from a trachybasaltic magma enriched in K2O, Al2O3, and P2O5, and depleted in CaO. Successively, a hotter basaltic calcalkaline magma recharged the plumbing system causing reverse zoning and crystallization of olivine before eruption (Fig. 4).

5.2. Late MI reliability based on MIA sensu stricto

Late MI can be tested for their reliability in recording the silicate melt composition because they represent an MIA sensu stricto. Based on the Roedder's rules, a MI is reliable if it follows three basic rules: (1) the MI represents an isochoric system, (2) nothing is added or lost from the MI, and (3) the MI originally trapped a homogeneous phase. To test these three rules, we studied MI trapped simultaneously and, thus, at the same chemical and physical conditions (MI of a single MIA, Bodnar and Student, 2006). If more than one MI within a single MIA show the same phase(s) at room T or consistent ratios of glass to bubble, and show the same composition, then we can be confident that these MI are reliable. In our case, the late MIA contains MI that show the same phase at room T. In fact, late MI > 22 Î¼m are all homogeneous glasses with the same chemical compositions (within analytical errors). This test shows that MI larger than â‰¥22 Î¼m are reliable because they follow Roedder's rule. The fidelity of MI is also supported by the degree of PEC decreasing from 5 to 2% as the size of the MI within the same MIA increases from 12 to 34 Î¼m (Fig. 3c). In addition, we noted that no correlation between the ratio of slow versus fast diffusing elements could be observed (e.g., P2O5/Na2O Fig. 3d). This suggests that the correlation between specific element concentrations and size is not caused by a boundary layer developed as MI were being trapped. Instead, these size versus concentration profiles suggest the presence of a boundary layer driven by PEC at the MI/olivine interface after trapping and before eruption (see Newcombe et al., 2014).

5.3. Reliability of P2O5 concentration of MI

The comparison between MI and bulk rocks does not show that MI are enriched in slow diffusing element such as P2O5 and Al2O3 relative to bulk rocks (Supplementary Fig. 1). This suggests that the P boundary layer at the melt-olivine interface and at the time of MI entrapment is not significant even though undoubtedly present. We interpret the lack of P-enrichment in MI to signify that the time to include an MI (10â€“35 Î¼m in diameter) entirely is longer than the growth of an olivine band highly enriched in P (generally 1â€“3 Î¼m). That MI are trapped during periods between rapid growthâ€”as opposed to during rapid growthâ€” is supported by some of the MI being located between two thin (1 Î¼m) P-rich zones (Fig. 1 B, intermediate MI).

6. Conclusions

For this study, we selected a single olivine from a base surge deposit of Solchiaro on the Island of Procida (Southern Italy). We selected this single olivine because it hosted many glassy MI and was inversely zoned for FeMg. We assessed the relative age of each individual MI studied to investigate the order of appearance of specific types of basalts within a single plumbing system (Fig. 4) and the fidelity of MI as a tool to understand magmatic processes. In our study, three populations of MI are recognized based on optical microscopy, P X-ray mapping, and major element compositions. We showed that early MI have originated from K-rich trachybasaltic magma (t0-t1, Fig. 4). Subsequentially (t1, Fig. 4), intermediate MI represent the result of simple fractional crystallization from the trachybasaltic magma. Finally, late MI recorded a magma mixing event between a hotter recharging calcalkaline basaltic magma and a pre-existing trachybasaltic magma (t2, Fig. 4) before triggering the Solchiaro eruption (t3, Fig. 4). The late MI represent an MIA sensu stricto, and we studied this MIA to understand that those â‰¥22 Î¼m are the most reliable to record the composition of the melt during olivine crystal growth.
