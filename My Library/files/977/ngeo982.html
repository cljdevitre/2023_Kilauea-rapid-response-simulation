Episodic swell growth inferred from variable uplift of the Cape Verde hotspot islands

Abstract

On the Beagle voyage, Charles Darwin first noted the creation and subsidence of ocean islands1, establishing in geology’s infancy that island freeboard changes with time. Hotspot ocean islands have an obvious mechanism for freeboard change through the growth of the bathymetric anomaly, or swell2, on which the islands rest. Models for swell development indicate that flexural9, thermal2,3 or dynamic pressure4,5,6,8 contributions, as well as spreading of melt residue from the hotspot7, can all contribute to island uplift. Here we test various models for swell development using the uplift histories for the islands of the Cape Verde hotspot, derived from isotopic dating of marine terraces and subaerial to submarine lava-flow morphologies. The island uplift histories, in conjunction with inter-island spacing, uplift rate and timing differences, rule out flexural, thermal or dynamic pressure contributions. We also find that uplift cannot be reconciled with models that advocate the spreading of melt residue in swell development unless swell growth is episodic. Instead, we infer from the uplift histories that two processes have acted to raise the islands during the past 6 Myr. During an initial phase, mantle processes acted to build the swell. Subsequently, magmatic intrusions at the island edifice caused 350 m of local uplift at the scale of individual islands. Finally, swell-wide uplift contributed a further 100 m of surface rise.

Main

Hotspots are enigmatic for many reasons, one of which is the unknown agent that forms the bathymetric swell underlying the islands. Three main possibilities are (1) thermal rejuvenation of the lithosphere and consequent expansion2,3, (2) dynamic pressure of buoyant plume material impinging on the base of the lithosphere4,5,6 and (3) accumulation and spreading of less-dense partial-melting residue to form the island chains7,10. Uplift histories of ocean islands resting on swells on stationary plates could provide useful constraints to assess which process dominates without the complexities due to a moving plate such as the Hawaiian hotspot prototype6,8. For example, synchronous uplift of all islands on a swell argues against the spreading residue idea, whereas variable uplift chronologies among islands disagrees with the idea of thermal rejuvenation or dynamic pressure. The Cape Verde islands (Fig. 1) feature abundant evidence of uplift11 in palaeo-sea-level markers that extend from present sea level up to 450 m and are radiometrically dateable (see Supplementary Methods), providing a record through the recent period of island building that is good enough to yield process constraints. Figure 2 shows the temporal uplift chronology determined on two islands with dateable markers: Santiago and São Nicolau.

The results reveal that the two islands experienced generally similar uplift trends, although at different rates or at different times. Santiago has experienced a constant nearly linear uplift of ∼100 m Myr−1, in the past 4 Myr. São Nicolau has experienced two uplift episodes, at 5.4–4.7 Myr and at 0.5 Myr—present. Its average uplift rate for 5.5 Myr is ∼35 m Myr−1, a more modest rate than for Santiago. These values are possibly an underestimate because the older palaeo-markers are eroded, but are comparable with the calculated rates for the Hawaiian Islands over the past 0.5 Myr (20–330 m Myr−1; ref. 9). The maximum vertical displacement we found is ∼450 m, comparable to the 400 m uplift inferred in ref. 12 using seismic reflection profiles and flexure calculations.

Assuming variable uplift, Fig. 2c shows that the data allow two different uplift periods to have occurred. The two islands might have been uplifted at rates of ∼150 m Myr−1 followed by quiescence, and then a final joint uplift of 100 m in 0.25–1.25 Myr (0.08–0.40 km Myr−1). The uncertainty in the final uplift duration stems from the variable sea-level curve over the past ∼1 Myr and our ignorance of whether the palaeo-sea-level markers correspond to highstands, lowstands or average sea level. Quaternary uplift evidence elsewhere in Cape Verde also suggests a recent uplift process that is regional in nature. All except for the youngest, westernmost islands feature wave-cut surfaces, marine terraces and lava deltas at up to ∼100 m asl (ref. 11).

To test the hypothesis that island uplift records the swell growth process, we obtain theoretical uplift rates from an analytical model for an axisymmetric gravity current13. This approach, used for an earlier model of the Cape Verde swell7, is justified because the shallow seismic structure of the Cape Verde islands14 suggests that a high-speed, low-density layer lies below the islands. The gravity current model gives the geometry and the evolution of a buoyant viscous fluid that ponds below a rigid surface, an approximation of the conditions arising after partial melting. The model includes elastic bending in response to the subsurface load. Model parameters are the density of the fertile mantle ρ = 3,300 kg m−3, water density ρw = 1,030 kg m−3, density contrast between the fertile mantle and the depleted root 30≤Δρ≤720 kg m−3, depleted swell root viscosity 1×1019≤η≤1×1021 Pa s and flux-rate parameter 0≤α≤1. To calculate the total swell volume we used a right circular disc (a good approximation to the gravity current shape; Fig. 3) that spreads to radial distance r∼600 km in 11.5 Myr. Feasible values that yield the observed swell volume, swell height and geoid anomaly are Δρ = 78 kg m−3, η = 1×1020 Pa s and α = 1. We calculate the corresponding swell topography with subsurface loading of an elastic plate with rigidity E = 100 GPa and elastic thickness Te = 30 km (refs 12, 15). The best-fitting combination yields an 8.1 m geoid anomaly, 2.1 km of swell bathymetry and a root thickness of 62 km as compared with observed values of 8–12 m, 1.9–2.3 km (refs 16, 17, 18) and 60–80 km (ref. 14), respectively. Figure 3 shows the swell root shape and predicted uplift rates at various distances and times from the spreading origin.

The observed island uplift rates are not compatible with this model of swell development. On the basis of the cessation of riverine sediment deposition on the Cape Verde Rise12,19 and magmatism ages20, swell development began at 17–26 Myr. For São Nicolau and Santiago to have continuous uplift rates as high as those observed, they would have to be 300–700 km from the swell centre, yet they are <150 km away (Figs 1 and 3). This implies that the uplift is not due to sequenced, progressive uplift from a spreading swell source.

The flexural response from the loads imposed on a plate by the volcanic piles built during nearby island development, or by giant mass wasting events in island edifices, is another mechanism that could yield sequenced uplift21,22. We find that the flexural parameters appropriate for Cape Verde do not permit significant differential uplift because the islands sit in one another’s flexural moats (see Supplementary Methods). There is also no bathymetric evidence of any mass wasting event of sufficient scale to effect differential uplift as large as that observed15.

Consequently, neither swell-scale nor regional processes yield the observed patterns and magnitudes of uplift that we observe. Over durations of 4 Myr (Santiago) or 5.5 Myr (São Nicolau), uplift rates of 0.1–0.035 km Myr−1 are feasible at ages of 4–5 Myr (Fig. 3). Short-term rates of up to 0.15 km Myr−1 over the ∼2 Myr intervals suggested in Fig. 2c are also feasible, as well as the last, at 0.25–1.25 Myr (Fig. 3). However, these must be late additions to the swell, requiring the buoyancy source to be intermittent.

These observations suggest that at least two processes cooperatively act to raise the sea floor under ocean islands. One process must locally act at the scale of an individual island to yield extra uplift. We invoke laccoliths formed under each island base to explain this phenomenon23. It has been shown24 that laccoliths form at rigidity contrasts between materials, such as the one between the seafloor sediments and the extrusive volcanics at the base of the island edifice. For plausible rigidities and magmatic intrusion pressures, laccolith thicknesses of 200–350 m can be emplaced in the edifices, covering diameters of 5–7 km, significant portions of the subaerial island areas (see Supplementary Methods). Dykes and sills are other possible uplift mechanisms, but their narrower extent would create tilting of formerly level palaeosurfaces such as raised beaches and phreatomagmatic crater floors, and create lopsided cones, and monoclines. These are not recognized features on ocean islands in general, or of Cape Verde11. On the contrary, the geometry of subaerial/submarine passage zones shown by the Cape Verde lava deltas is subhorizontal and thus incompatible with the tilting of sequences. This justifies invoking laccoliths as the principal internal uplift mechanism. Indeed, Japanese arc volcanoes seem to be built on sections of uplifted arc basement25, and on Maio in Cape Verde a Jurassic seafloor carbonate enclave of a few km2 is exposed26, indicating that a process exists that is capable of raising formerly deep material to shallow levels.

The second process must act at the swell’s length scale, ∼1,600 km in the case of Cape Verde, related to building the swell. The signal attributable to this yields 100 m of uplift in 0.25–1.25 Myr. If it is responsible for building the 20 times higher swell, the archipelago must have taken 5–25 Myr to develop and must have developed incrementally. The timescale agrees with the ≥17 Myr swell development age, but not the episodicity. If built incrementally, the moat fill stratigraphy should contain more cyclic features; however, a single uplift event is inferred from present data12.

Of the three models of swell development, incremental or episodic uplift is not in accord with a dynamic pressure origin because it entails corresponding increments in pressure at the lithosphere’s base; accelerating volcanic output is not a feature associated with hotspot volcanism8,27. Long-term uplift accompanies thermal rejuvenation or spreading of melting residue, but thermal rejuvenation is ruled out because wave speeds in the shallow mantle are faster than in the average mantle14. Melt-residue spreading can yield episodic, time-progressive uplift, but this is not clearly observable in Cape Verde. The data suggest that the uplift process is intermittent, building the swell in an initial phase, with minor later incremental additions. Island uplift is due, in large part, to basal laccolith emplacement.
