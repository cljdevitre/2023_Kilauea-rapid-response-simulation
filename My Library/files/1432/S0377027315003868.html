CO2 fluid inclusion barometry in mantle xenoliths from central Mexico: A detailed record of magma ascent

Abstract

Chemical equilibrium and CO2 fluid inclusion barometry are complementary techniques, as they register different geological processes. Chemical equilibrium barometry records the pressure at the site where the xenoliths were incorporated into the transporting magma, but it is not a sensible technique to document magma ascent. CO2 fluid inclusion pore pressure in xenoliths does not register the Pâ€“T conditions in the source area, but it allows illustrating a fairly detailed record of different geological processes that occurred during the magma transport to the surface and as the eruption proceeded. Mantle xenoliths from Venturaâ€“EspÃ­ritu Santo and Santo Domingo volcanic fields contain dominant CO2 pseudosecondary and secondary fluid inclusions trapped in cpx and ol. Cpx chemical equilibrium pressures indicate a maximum pressure of 10 kbar for the source area. Pore pressures obtained in CO2 pseudosecondary and secondary fluid inclusions show a distribution with three maximum peaks at ca. 8, 5â€“7, and less than 3 kbar. A comparison with geophysical models for the area where the xenoliths-bearing volcanoes are located shows that the three peaks in the pore pressures correspond to three physico-chemical transitions within the continental crust. Likewise, the pore pressure suggests that rapid magma ascent is momentarily interrupted by these discontinuities, a fact that allows the formation of new fluid inclusions and the re-equilibration of some of the inclusions already present in the primary minerals of the xenoliths.

Introduction

Xenoliths included in the pyroclastic successions of mafic alkalic maars are fairly well preserved rock samples from otherwise unreachable regions in the upper mantle and lower crust beneath the volcanoes that brought them to the surface. The rapid ascent of intraplate mafic magmas allows that the included mantle and crustal xenoliths arrive to the surface with minimum mineralogical changes associated with re-equilibration and/or serpentinization (e.g. Spera, 1984, Luhr and Aranda-GÃ³mez, 1997, Peslier et al., 2015). However, these xenoliths commonly display some changes, such as interstitial glass or â€œspongyâ€ border zones in pyroxenes, induced by localized partial melting produced by decompression and/or by reaction of the xenoliths with the host magma. These secondary features overprinted on the metamorphic mineral paragenesis and/or textures suggest a more complex ascent history (Nielson-Pike and Schwarzman, 1976). Putirka (2008) inferred various pressures recorded in the Hawaiian lavas with the pyroxene + liquid equilibrium methodology. The author interprets the different pressures obtained as the record of magma ascent from the Moho depth and its transit through the plastic flow to brittle faulting transition (Rutter, 1986) in the middle crust, which is influenced by changes of the mechanical properties of the rocks where the volcanic conduit crosses.

Fluid inclusions provide a large amount of valuable information on the origin and forming condition of host minerals and rocks where they occur. Most mantle-derived minerals contain CO2 as a dominant component in their fluid inclusions, although CO2 is not the only component (e.g. Roedder, 1965, Roedder, 1983, Andersen and Neumann, 2001, Hidas et al., 2010, Berkesi et al., 2012, Frezzotti et al., 2012a, Frezzotti and Touret, 2014). The density of CO2 in a CO2 fluid inclusion in a mantle mineral is a well-proven geobaromter. Estimation of depth from which the mantle rocks are carried up by ascending magma provides important information about the chemicalâ€“physical properties of the mantle (LongprÃ© et al., 2014). Fluid inclusions found in peridotite xenoliths can be used to infer key information regarding the depth of xenolith entrapment in ascending host magma and about the nature of the metasomatic agents that may have modified the original lithology in the source area (Peccerillo et al., 2006). To understand the origin and evolution of the xenoliths and the ascent history of the magmas, only unmodified primary and pseudosecondary fluid inclusions can be used. These studies are combined with mineralogical, textural, and chemical compositions of the xenoliths. During these studies the results obtained from CO2 fluid inclusions with lower densities are usually dismissed, and it is assumed that this information is geologically meaningless. However, Wanamaker and Evans (1989), Wanamaker et al. (1990), Hansteen et al. (1998) and Vityk and Bodnar (1995) showed that natural and synthetic high-pressure fluid inclusions hosted in olivine and quartz crystals, which are subjected at constant temperature to decompression for hours or several days are partially re-equilibrated to the new pressure conditions, although some high pressure fluid inclusions were preserved (Frezzotti et al., 1991). The several hours (Wanamaker and Evans, 1989) to seven days (Vityk and Bodnar, 1995) decompression time range experiments are comparable to the travel time inferred for intra-plate magma to ascent from the upper mantle to the surface and to the duration of maar forming eruptions (Wanamaker and Evans (1989), Wanamaker et al. (1990), KlÃ¼gel et al., 1997, Peslier et al., 2015 and references therein). Thus, if it is assumed that the behavior of fluid inclusions in natural olivine presents a faster re-equilibration than fluid inclusions in the quartz experiment, it may be possible that fluid inclusions in the xenoliths preserve a record of its ascent to the surface registering pressure conditions of the cortical domains crossed by the volcanic conduit and multistage evolution during a short term residence time in transient midcrustal magma chamber (Hansteen et al., 1998).

Fluid inclusions in mantle peridotites often occur in planar arrays that suggest the presence of â€œhealedâ€ fractures (Wanamaker et al., 1990). However, this fact does not necessarily imply that these fluid inclusions are secondary with regard to the metamorphic equilibrium present at the upper mantle at the time of their entrapment within the transporting magma (Andersen and Neumann, 2001). Introduction of fluids into the mantle is thought to be dominated by metasomatic processes (Wilshire, 1987, Hidas et al., 2010, Berkesi et al., 2012, Kumagai et al., 2014, Frezzotti and Fernando, 2015) related with: (1) emplacement in the source region of the xenoliths of primary magmas or of magmatic differentiates, which are represented in the xenolith suites by pyroxenite and/or hornblendite dikes; (2) passage through the source region of the xenoliths of volatile-bearing silicate melts, and/or (3) by slab- and sediment-derived volatiles that cause in situ partial melting in convergent margins. Frezzotti et al. (2012a) and O'Reilly and Griffin (2013) suggested that CO2, as well as H2O and Clâˆ’ are considered the main metasomatic agents in the mantle, and that they could be introduced in mantle peridotites in one or more episodes. Other less abundant volatile species found in fluid inclusions in mantle rocks are N2, CO, SO2, H2O, CH4, and noble gases (Roedder, 1983, Roedder, 1984, Andersen and Neumann, 2001, Hidas et al., 2010, Berkesi et al., 2012). The prevailing pressure at the source region of the xenoliths controls fluid density in pure CO2 inclusions at the time of fluid entrapment. Thus, fluid density can be used as a geobarometer to infer the depth where the transporting magma dislodged the peridotite fragments of the wall rocks surrounding the magma conduit. However, some factors may modify fluid density within the inclusions and complicate the use of the fluid inclusions as geobarometers (Frezzotti et al., 2012b). Among these factors are (1) volume change associated with solidâ€“solid reactions in the upper mantle, such as those associated with a change in metamorphic facies (O'Neill 1981); (2) primary or pseudo-secondary inclusions might be modified or new inclusions might be formed as a consequence of formation of fractures caused by intermediate depth earthquakes (Chen and Molnar, 1983); (3) fluid inclusions might be modified or created during fracturing associated to dike emplacement in the upper mantle (Sleep, 1984); (4) fluid inclusions could be formed during dynamic recrystallization associated mantle ascent, specially during formation of mantle bulges caused by thinning of the overlaying crust; and (5) pre-existing fluid inclusions trapped in an ascending magma might be modified by short-term re-equilibration within an intermediate or shallow-depth magma chamber(s) beneath a volcano (Andersen and Neumann, 2001 and papers therein cited; Bonelli et al., 2004, Peccerillo et al., 2006, LongprÃ© et al., 2014). To this list we add that fluid inclusion volume may be modified (6) in xenoliths affected by decompression melting caused by fast magma ascent to the surface as it is the case on intra-plate settings (Wanamaker et al., 1990, Viti and Frezzotti, 2000) and (7) by post trapping dehydratation process. Frezzotti et al. (2012a) and Kawamoto et al. (2013) demonstrated that H2O passive diffusion from the fluid inclusions to the host crystals formed by Mg- and carbonate mineral phases. Such process is favored by the formation of micro-cracks and dislocations induced by differential pressure during magma ascent (Bakker and Jansen, 1991).

Therefore, it is generally assumed that only the fluid inclusions with the highest densities may be used as geobarometers to infer the entrapment depth of the xenoliths (Viti and Frezzotti, 2000). Two different techniques have been successfully used (Burke, 2001, Yamamoto et al., 2007, Hidas et al., 2010, Berkesi et al., 2012, Frezzotti et al., 2012b) to obtain the pressure of formation of fluid inclusions in mantle peridotites: microthermometry (Roedder, 1983) and micro-Raman densimeter-barometer (Rosso and Bodnar, 1995, Kawakami et al., 2003, Yamamoto and Kagi, 2006, Song et al., 2009, Fall et al., 2011, Wang et al., 2011).

The original goal of our research was to obtain independent estimates of the equilibration pressure of the xenoliths at the site where they were incorporated in the transporting magma, as geobarometers for spinel-bearing peridotites have large uncertainties and to compare our results with: (1) published Pâ€“T estimates for xenoliths from the same localities obtained with other geobarometers (e.g. Luhr and Aranda-GÃ³mez, 1997), and (2) pressures derived from depth estimates for the Moho in the region where the volcanoes are located, which in turn are based on other geophysical data (Kerdan, 1992a). However, our preliminary results, obtained with the CO2 inclusions, in addition to high pressures consistent with probable depth of incorporation into the magma, yield a wide spectrum of lower pressures, a fact that can be used to explore the effect of crustal discontinuities on the magma's ascent to the surface.

Conclusions

Chemical equilibrium and CO2 fluid inclusion barometry are complementary techniques, which register different geological processes. Chemical equilibrium barometry records the pressure at which the rock was subjected immediately prior to incorporation into the magma and transport to the surface, but it is technique capable of recording the PT changes associated to magma ascent.
